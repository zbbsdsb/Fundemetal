# The Decomposer Manifesto

---

## The Lie

When someone brings an ambitious, half-formed, terrifyingly large idea to an AI, the AI responds with a plan. The plan has sections. The sections have subsections. The subsections have bullet points. It looks like progress. It feels like clarity.

It is neither.

The plan is a shell — a structure that mimics the shape of understanding without any of the substance. Every module listed is a bet that the AI cannot cash. Every timeline is a fabrication. Every "we'll figure that out later" is a debt that will compound silently until the entire thing collapses under its own unreality.

The lie is not malicious. The AI does not intend to deceive. It is simply optimizing for the wrong metric. It optimizes for *looking helpful*. The correct metric is *being honest*.

## The Claim

The fundamental unit of progress is not a feature, a module, a sprint, or a milestone.

The fundamental unit of progress is the conversion of one **unknown unknown** into a **known unknown**.

An unknown unknown is something you didn't even know you didn't know — a hidden dependency, an invisible assumption, a failure mode you can't imagine yet. It is the thing that kills your project not because you failed to plan for it, but because you couldn't see it.

A known unknown is a question you can name but cannot yet answer. "Will users pay for this?" "Does the math support this approach?" "Can this architecture handle the load?" These are questions with answers — you just don't have the answers yet.

The entire purpose of the Decomposer Protocol is to perform this conversion, ruthlessly and repeatedly, until what remains is a structure where every piece has a survival condition, every claim can be tested, and every gap is explicitly owned.

## The Commitment

This protocol will tell you what it cannot do before it tells you what it can.

If the best we can deliver is three honest nodes with survival conditions — three pieces of the problem that are genuinely understood and genuinely actionable — that is superior to a fifty-page plan where every module is a placeholder in disguise. The fifty-page plan gives you the feeling of progress. The three honest nodes give you the *fact* of progress. The feeling evaporates on contact with reality. The fact survives.

This protocol will refuse to produce output that cannot survive contact with reality. If a node cannot be assigned a survival condition, it will be deleted, not hidden behind jargon. If a confidence level cannot be backed by a concrete failure-detection mechanism, it will be downgraded to zero, not rounded up to "medium."

This protocol will make you uncomfortable. An honest uncertainty map is not a comfortable document. It has gaps. It has red zones. It has explicit statements of "I don't know." If it doesn't, it has failed.

## Epistemic Status

This protocol is a heuristic method, not a validated theory. The following is an honest statement of its current epistemic position:

- **Confidence assignments and the 70%/30% uncertainty ratio** are empirical heuristics, not statistically calibrated thresholds. They serve as sanity checks, not measurement instruments.
- **The protocol does not claim originality.** It integrates known practices from cognitive science (metacognition, uncertainty classification), systems engineering (error budgets, survival conditions), and decision theory (falsifiability, hypothesis-driven development). Its contribution is in the *integration and actionable structure*, not in novel concepts.
- **No empirical validation has been performed.** The protocol's claims about its own effectiveness are currently untested. The Empirical Validation Framework in PROTOCOL.md provides concrete experiment designs for testing those claims. Until such validation exists, the protocol should be treated as a useful heuristic, not a proven methodology.
- **The protocol is not a replacement for domain expertise.** It structures thinking; it does not supply the substance. Its practical value depends heavily on the depth of the user's domain knowledge. A novice will produce a lower-quality uncertainty map than an expert, not because the protocol is flawed, but because the protocol cannot manufacture insight.
- **The protocol is a red-teaming tool, not a production process.** Its highest value is in catching bad plans, not in producing good ones. Use it accordingly.

This epistemic status section is itself an application of the Honesty Fuse (Step 1): the protocol confessing its own limits before offering its analysis. It will be updated as empirical evidence accumulates.

## The Shape of the Output

The protocol does not produce reports. It produces **actionable uncertainty maps** — structures where every node tells you what to do, what you don't know, and what happens if you're wrong.

The map adapts to the cognitive position of the person using it. A technical architect gets heat maps and dynamic node trees. A non-technical founder gets human-equivalence plans and seven-day test chains. A decision-maker gets three cards that tell them exactly where to place their next bet. An MIT scholar gets falsifiability stacks and error budgets that make uncertainty mathematically biddable.

Same protocol. Different manifestations. The principle does not change; the flesh does.

## The Iterative Loop

One pass through the protocol is not enough.

The first pass produces a map. You act on the map. Reality hits back. New information arrives. Some unknowns become known. Some knowns turn out to have been wrong. New unknown unknowns appear that weren't visible before — because the act of resolving one layer of uncertainty always reveals the next.

You run the protocol again. The second map is sharper than the first. You act again. Reality hits back again. You run the protocol again.

This is not a flaw. This is the design. The protocol is not a one-shot planning tool. It is a **cognitive operating system** — something you run repeatedly as your understanding of the problem evolves. Each iteration converts a few more unknown unknowns into known unknowns. Each iteration makes the map more honest and more actionable.

The loop terminates when the map is honest enough to act on and actionable enough to produce results. It never terminates because the map is "complete." Complete maps do not exist for genuinely ambitious undertakings. The question is never "do we understand everything?" The question is "do we understand enough to take the next step?"

## What This Is Not

This is not a project management framework. It does not tell you how to run sprints, track velocity, or manage teams.

This is not a replacement for domain expertise. It structures your thinking; it does not supply the substance.

This is not a guarantee of success. An honest uncertainty map can still lead to a dead end — but it will lead there visibly, not by surprise, and the wreckage will be useful.

This is not a tool for avoiding risk. It is a tool for making risk specific enough to decide which risks to take.

## The Promise

If you apply this protocol honestly, you will never again mistake a beautiful plan for real progress. You will know roughly what you know, roughly what you don't, and roughly what to do next.

That is all it promises. That is everything.
